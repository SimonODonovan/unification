🏭  First evening — Old Town orientation
Royal Mile closes & Grassmarket
📍  Royal Mile, Edinburgh EH1  ·  Hotel front door
Free ⏱  45 min walk, then dinner

Walk south-west from the hotel along the Royal Mile toward Grassmarket. The route takes you down the Lawnmarket and off down Victoria Street — a curved, colourful medieval street that drops into the Grassmarket square at the foot of Castle Rock. Explore two or three closes (alleyways) as you go.

  • Advocates Close: Steps down from the Royal Mile with views of the city below and Princes Street Gardens. Atmospheric in the early evening light.
  • Dunbar’s Close Garden: A hidden 17th-century walled garden tucked behind a close door on the Mile. Free to enter, usually quiet in the early evening — one of Edinburgh’s small secrets.
  • Victoria Street: Turn left off the Mile down George IV Bridge, then left again onto the curve of Victoria Street. Independent shops line both levels of the street — most are open until 5:30–6pm on a Saturday, so arriving at 4:30pm gives a good hour of browsing before dinner. The view back up the curve from the Grassmarket end is the best angle on the street.
  • Grassmarket: A wide cobbled square at the foot of the castle rock. Busy on a Saturday evening with a good range of restaurants on all sides. Armstrong’s Vintage is on the Grassmarket itself — see thrift note below.
Shop hours note: If you check in and leave the hotel at 4–5pm, you have about 1–1.5 hours of shopping time before most independent shops close at 5:30–6pm. Victoria Street boutiques and the Grassmarket shops are your best bet for early-evening browsing. Restaurants in the Grassmarket begin service from 5:30pm, so the timing flows naturally: browse then eat.
Thrift note: Armstrong’s Vintage (83 Grassmarket, EH1 2HJ) is one of the best vintage and second-hand clothing shops in Scotland — a large, well-organised space with a huge range of clothing, accessories, and costumes at good prices. Open until 5:30pm on Saturdays. Right on the Grassmarket square, hard to miss. A priority stop on the way to dinner.
Romantic note: Victoria Street at dusk is one of the most photographed streets in Scotland for good reason. The curve of different-coloured buildings, the cobbles, and the castle visible above the rooftops at the Grassmarket end — this is a genuinely lovely first evening in Edinburgh. No plan needed beyond walking slowly and seeing where each close leads.

Day 9
Edinburgh Castle & Old Town
Monday, 14 September 2026
Morning — the castle
🏛  Castle — pre-book timed entry
Edinburgh Castle
📍  Castlehill, Edinburgh EH1 2NG  ·  10-min walk uphill from hotel
💲  ~£22pp  |  Book: edinburghcastle.scot 🕐  Open 9:30am daily ⏱  Allow 2.5–3hrs

Built on a volcanic plug of rock that rises 130m above the city, the castle dominates Edinburgh from every angle. There is considerably more inside than most visitors expect. Pre-book a timed entry — September dates sell out.

  • The Scottish Crown Jewels (Honours of Scotland): The oldest crown jewels in the British Isles — the crown, sword, and sceptre of Scotland, used at coronations since the 15th century.
  • The Stone of Destiny: The ancient coronation stone on which Scottish (and later British) monarchs were crowned. It was held in Westminster Abbey for 700 years and returned to Scotland in 1996.
  • The One O’Clock Gun: Fired daily at 1pm (Monday–Saturday) from the Half Moon Battery. A tradition since 1861, originally to help ships in the Firth of Forth set their chronometers. Stand on the esplanade at 12:58pm to be in position.
  • The Great Hall: A 16th-century banqueting hall with one of the finest original timber hammer-beam roofs in Scotland. The armour displays are remarkable.
  • Mons Meg: A medieval siege cannon from 1449, one of the largest medieval guns in existence. It is outside the Great Hall — the barrel is 15 feet long.
  • The views: From the Half Moon Battery, 360° views of the city — Arthur’s Seat, the Firth of Forth, the New Town, and south to the Pentland Hills.
Strategy: Arrive at opening (9:30am) to avoid queues at the Crown Jewels (they build up fast). Do the Crown Jewels first, then the Great Hall, then walk the ramparts. Use the included audio guide for the Crown Jewels room — it adds significant context.

1 min walk
🔭  Victorian optical experience — pre-book
Camera Obscura & World of Illusions
📍  Castlehill, Edinburgh EH1 2ND  ·  1-min walk from Edinburgh Castle exit
💲  ~£20pp  ·  camera-obscura.co.uk 🕐  9:30am–9pm daily ⏱  Allow 1–1.5hrs

A Victorian optical tower that has operated since 1853, built directly above the Old Town on Castlehill. The rooftop Camera Obscura itself projects a live real-time image of Edinburgh onto a white dish — you can watch people walking on Princes Street below, in silence, in a darkened room. Below the roof are five floors of optical illusions, holograms, and interactive exhibits. Nothing like it anywhere else in Edinburgh.

  • The Camera Obscura (roof): A rotating mirror and lens projects a live panorama of Edinburgh onto a concave white dish. The guide can pick up individual people from the street and “hold” them in the palm of their hand. It is genuinely startling and rather beautiful. Allow 20 minutes here.
  • The illusions floors: Five floors of well-curated optical illusions — Ames rooms, vortex tunnels, giant mirror mazes, and Victorian scientific instruments. Better than the description suggests — a lot of it is genuinely fun to experience together.
  • The rooftop terrace: Free to access during your visit — 360° views over the Old Town, equal to Calton Hill from a different angle. A good photograph from up here.
Romantic note: The Camera Obscura room itself is a surprisingly intimate experience — a dark, quiet room with a live moving image of the city projected below you. One of those things that is difficult to describe but very pleasant to experience together.

Afternoon — Old Town
⛪  Cathedral — free
St Giles’ Cathedral
📍  High Street, Edinburgh EH1 1RE  ·  On the Royal Mile, 5-min walk from castle
Free  entry 🕐  Mon–Fri 9am–7pm, Sat 9am–5pm, Sun 1pm–5pm ⏱  30–45 min

The High Kirk of Edinburgh — the church at the centre of Scottish religious history. The nave is impressive, but the Thistle Chapel at the far end is extraordinary: a tiny, ornately carved chapel crammed with heraldic detail.

  • The Thistle Chapel (1911): Scotland’s equivalent of the Order of the Garter chapel in Windsor. 16 stalls, each one carved with the personal heraldry of its knight. The craftsmanship is astonishing in a space this small.
  • The John Knox statue: The reformer who shaped Scottish Protestantism stands in one of the side chapels. His pulpit is preserved here.
  • The Parliament Square side: Exit through the south door to Parliament Square — the old Scottish Parliament building (now law courts) and the mercat cross where royal proclamations were made.

🏭  Street & graveyard — free
Victoria Street & Greyfriars Kirkyard
📍  Greyfriars Pl, Edinburgh EH1 2QQ  ·  5-min walk south of the Royal Mile
Free ⏱  45–60 min for both

Victoria Street is the curved, colourful medieval street most often cited as an inspiration for Diagon Alley. Greyfriars Kirkyard below it is one of the most atmospheric graveyards in Europe.

  • Victoria Street: Walk slowly. The buildings are 3–4 storeys of different-coloured painted stone. Independent shops on both levels. The view back up the curve of the street is particularly good.
  • Greyfriars Bobby statue: At the top of Candlemaker Row — the bronze terrier said to have guarded his owner’s grave for 14 years. The nose is worn bright from being touched by tourists.
  • Greyfriars Kirkyard: The graveyard itself is free and open. The covenanter’s prison (an outdoor enclosure) is in the far corner. Many of the names on the gravestones appear in the Harry Potter books.
  • George Mackenzie mausoleum: The baroque round tomb of the man who imprisoned the Covenanters. A focal point of the graveyard’s dark history.

Evening — Calton Hill at sunset
⛰  Viewpoint — free, 5-min climb
Calton Hill
📍  Calton Hill, Edinburgh EH7 5AA  ·  East end of Princes Street
Free ⏱  5 min climb, 30–45 min on top 🕐  Open at all times

A small hill at the east end of Princes Street with a 5-minute path to the summit. The 360° views from the top are arguably the best free view in Edinburgh — castle, Arthur’s Seat, the Firth of Forth, the New Town grid, the Old Town skyline all visible at once. Especially good at dusk in September.

  • National Monument: A partial replica of the Parthenon, begun in 1826 and never finished due to lack of funds. Known locally as “Scotland’s Disgrace.” Striking against the sky.
  • Nelson Monument: A tower with a time ball that drops at 1pm daily (same tradition as the Castle gun). You can climb it for a small fee.
  • The view west: The castle, the spire of the Balmoral Hotel, Arthur’s Seat behind it — the whole of Edinburgh’s skyline from the east. The light at 7–8pm in September is warm and long.
Getting there: Walk east from the Royal Mile along Canongate, then north up Calton Road, then up the steps to the hill. About 15 min from the hotel. Or walk along Princes Street to the east end and take the path up from Waterloo Place.
Romantic note: Calton Hill at sunset in September is one of the most reliably beautiful things Edinburgh offers. The light turns the castle and the New Town golden from the east, and the hill is rarely as crowded as the daytime. Bring a layer — it is always cooler up top than in the city. Sit on the grass facing west and watch the light change. No agenda beyond this.
Thrift note: On the walk to Calton Hill from the Royal Mile, Herman Brown (151 West Port, EH3 9DP) is a short detour west — one of Edinburgh’s best independent vintage clothing shops, well-curated men’s and women’s, reasonable prices. Or keep it for another day; West Port is on the way between Grassmarket and the Meadows.

Day 10
Arthur’s Seat, Chocolatarium & Botanic Garden
Tuesday, 15 September 2026
Early morning — start by 8am
⛰  Hike — free, allow 1.5–2hrs
Arthur’s Seat
📍  Holyrood Park, Edinburgh EH8 8HG  ·  20-min walk from hotel
Free ⏱  45 min to summit, 30 min down  ·  total 1.5–2hrs 🕐  Open at all times — start early

An ancient volcano in the middle of the city, rising 251m above sea level. The views from the summit are the finest in Edinburgh by a considerable margin. The climb is not technically difficult — good footwear is sufficient. Start early on a Tuesday to have the path mostly to yourself.

  • Getting to the start: Walk east down the Royal Mile, past Holyrood Palace, into Holyrood Park. The main path to the summit (Volunteer’s Walk) starts from the car park near St Margaret’s Loch. About 20 min walk from the hotel.
  • The path: Follow the wide path south-west from St Margaret’s Loch. It climbs steadily through open grassland — no scrambling required on the main route. The ridge becomes steeper near the top. Allow 45 min at a comfortable pace.
  • The summit: 360° views — the full Edinburgh skyline to the west, the Firth of Forth and Fife to the north, the Pentland Hills to the south, the North Sea to the east. On a clear September morning this is extraordinary.
  • Wind: It is almost always windy at the top. Bring a layer even if it is warm in the city below.
  • Descent: Return by the same path, or take the alternate route down past Salisbury Crags (the dramatic cliff face on the west side) — adds 20 min but spectacular.
Practical: Wear trainers with a decent grip — the path is rocky near the top. There are no facilities on the hill. Bring water. The summit is exposed — September mornings can be cold even if Edinburgh below is mild.

🏛  Royal Palace — exterior
Palace of Holyroodhouse
📍  Canongate, Edinburgh EH8 8DX  ·  Foot of the Royal Mile, at the park entrance
Free  exterior  |  💲  ~£18pp for the interior 🕐  9:30am–4:30pm (check seasonal hours) ⏱  20 min exterior / 1.5hrs interior

The official Scottish residence of the monarch, at the foot of the Royal Mile directly opposite the Scottish Parliament. You will pass it on the return from Arthur’s Seat. It is primarily a historic royal palace rather than a garden or nature space — but the Abbey ruins attached to it are genuinely dramatic and free to see from outside the gates. Allow 15–20 minutes here rather than a full visit, then continue up the Royal Mile.

  • Holyrood Abbey ruins (exterior, free): The roofless shell of a 12th-century Augustinian abbey is attached to the right side of the palace and visible from the forecourt area. The arched window frames and the nave walls still standing against the sky are more visually dramatic than the palace itself — one of the best ruins in the city.
  • The palace forecourt: The baroque facade is impressive from the gate. The fountain in the forecourt dates from the 16th century. You do not need a ticket to see this — you can approach the gates freely.
  • Interior (paid, ~£18pp): The Mary Queen of Scots apartments, where she lived in the 1560s, are the most interesting rooms inside — they are small, surprisingly intimate, and contain the actual chamber where her secretary was murdered in front of her. Worth it if this specific history appeals; not primarily a nature or garden experience.
  • Nature note: The palace garden is not generally open to the public. For a genuine garden experience, the Royal Botanic Garden in Inverleith is the right choice — on the afternoon programme below.

🍫  Chocolate experience — pre-book recommended
The Chocolatarium
📍  11 Holyrood Road, Edinburgh EH8 8AE  ·  Just off the lower Royal Mile
💲  ~£20–30pp  ·  chocolatarium.com 🕐  Sessions run throughout the day — book a slot ⏱  ~1 hour

A chocolate-making and tasting experience in the heart of Edinburgh’s Old Town. Sessions walk you through the origins and production of Scottish chocolate, followed by hands-on chocolate making and tasting. Small, intimate, and genuinely good — a pleasant way to spend an hour after the Arthur’s Seat hike, and considerably more interesting than it sounds on paper.

  • The tasting session: A guided tasting of Scottish artisan chocolates — single-origin, flavoured, and seasonal varieties. Not a children’s party activity; well put together for adults.
  • Making your own: Most sessions include a hands-on element where you make a small chocolate to take away. A nice memento.
  • Scottish chocolate heritage: The history of Edinburgh’s chocolate industry (Rowntree, Cadbury connections, Victorian confectionery) is genuinely interesting background to the session.
Romantic note: Chocolate tasting together is a quietly enjoyable way to spend an hour — low-key, warm, and a good contrast to the morning’s outdoor hike. Book the morning session to start your walk to the Botanic Garden afterward.

🌿  Botanic garden — free entry to grounds
Royal Botanic Garden Edinburgh
📍  20A Inverleith Row, Edinburgh EH3 5LR  ·  Inverleith, north of the New Town
Free  grounds  |  💲  Glasshouses: ~£7pp 🕐  10am–6pm daily in September ⏱  Allow 1.5–2hrs

Twenty-eight hectares of landscaped gardens on the northern edge of the New Town. Founded in 1670, it is one of the oldest botanic gardens in Britain and completely free to walk. The main garden has lawns, a rock garden, a Chinese hillside garden, and a walled garden — and the view south from the upper terrace across the city to Edinburgh Castle is one of the finest in Edinburgh from anywhere at ground level.

  • The Edinburgh Castle view: From the upper terrace of the garden, looking south, Edinburgh Castle sits on its rock above the city skyline with the Pentland Hills behind. The garden is one of the very few places in Edinburgh where this panorama opens up at ground level without climbing a hill. A remarkable view to stumble across.
  • The Chinese Hillside: A recently developed section with winding paths through Chinese native plants, small waterfalls, and a distinctly different atmosphere from the formal lawns. Quiet and well worth exploring.
  • The Rock Garden: A large alpine garden with thousands of plant species arranged across a slope of natural stone. At its best in late September with autumn colour beginning.
  • The Victorian Glasshouses (paid, ~£7pp): Ten interconnected glasshouses including a tropical house, a temperate palm house, and an aquatic house. Worth the small charge — the Tropical Aquatic House in particular is impressive in scale.
  • The Terrace Cafe: Inside the garden, overlooking the main lawn. Good coffee and lunch options, with outdoor seating when the weather is reasonable.
Romantic note: The garden in September has a particular quality — the crowds of summer are gone, the light is lower and golden in the afternoon, and the lawns are still green. The view of the castle from the upper terrace is genuinely arresting. A slow walk through the Chinese Hillside section together, with no agenda, is one of the best hours Edinburgh offers.
Thrift note (Stockbridge, on the walk back): The route from the garden back to the hotel passes through Stockbridge — one of Edinburgh’s best neighbourhoods for independent shops. Hamilton Place and St Stephen Street have several vintage and second-hand shops worth browsing. In particular: Armstrongs on Teviot is accessible via Stockbridge on the walk south. Stockbridge market runs on Sundays (not Tuesday) but the shops are open all week.

Princes Street Gardens & New Town
📍  Princes Street Gardens, Edinburgh EH2 2AB  ·  North side of the Old Town
Free ⏱  1.5–2hrs

Princes Street Gardens runs along the base of the castle rock. The view looking up to Edinburgh Castle from the gardens is one of the most photographed in Scotland. The New Town beyond is a complete contrast to the Old Town — Georgian grid streets, grand terraces, a very different Edinburgh.

  • The gardens: A long park at the foot of the castle rock. The Ross Fountain, the Scott Monument (a Gothic spire to Sir Walter Scott — you can climb it for £12), and the flower clock.
  • The castle view: From the central path in the gardens, the castle sits directly above you on the rock. The best stationary photograph of Edinburgh Castle is from here.
  • George Street: A block north of Princes Street — the main shopping and restaurant street of the New Town. More relaxed and upmarket than Princes Street itself.
  • Rose Street: A pedestrian lane between Princes Street and George Street — bars, boutiques, good for a coffee stop.

Morning — light Old Town activity
🏭  Free museum — 5-min walk from hotel
National Museum of Scotland
📍  Chambers Street, Edinburgh EH1 1JF  ·  5-min walk from The Balmoral
Free  entry 🕐  Opens 10am daily ⏱  1.5–2hrs at a relaxed pace

One of the best free museums in the UK, and a 5-minute walk from the hotel. A good option for a departure morning — no booking needed, no pressure, and the Grand Gallery cafe inside is a good place for a final Edinburgh coffee. Go at opening (10am) and you will have a couple of hours before lunch.

  • The Grand Gallery: The Victorian cast-iron atrium at the centre of the building. Remarkable architecture and a natural first stop when you walk in.
  • Dolly the Sheep: The world’s first cloned mammal, preserved and on display. More moving in person than it sounds.
  • The Lewis Chessmen: Original 12th-century Norse chess pieces carved from walrus ivory, found on the Isle of Lewis. Extraordinarily expressive small figures — some of the most characterful medieval objects in any museum.
  • Scottish history galleries: The story of Scotland from Pictish stones through the Jacobite rising to the industrial revolution, laid out clearly across several floors.
Thrift note: The Chambers Street area and nearby Nicolson Street have several charity and vintage shops worth a final browse if you have time after the museum. In particular, Oxfam Edinburgh on Nicolson Street is well-stocked. About 5 min walk south from the museum entrance.
