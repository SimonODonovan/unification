Afternoon
🏭  Historic quarter — free to explore
Cathedral Quarter
📍  Cathedral Quarter, Belfast BT1  ·  Hotel front door
Free ⏱  45–60 min walk

The oldest surviving part of Belfast, centred on St Anne’s Cathedral. The narrow entries (Belfast’s historic alleyways) off Donegall Street and High Street are worth exploring. Very different character to anything seen on the trip so far.

  • The entries: Belfast’s equivalent of Edinburgh’s closes — narrow alleyways cutting between streets. Crown Entry, Pottinger’s Entry, and Joy’s Entry off High Street are the most atmospheric.
  • Commercial Court: A cobbled square lined with murals and independent bars. One of the most photographed spots in Belfast — the paving and painted walls are distinctive.
  • Writer’s Square: A modern public square in the Cathedral Quarter with benches and a good view of St Anne’s facade.
  • Hill Street and Exchange Street: The Cathedral Quarter’s main restaurant streets. Good for gauging options for dinner tonight and tomorrow.

⛪  Cathedral — free
St Anne’s Cathedral (Belfast Cathedral)
📍  Donegall Street, Belfast BT1 2HB  ·  2-min walk from hotel
Free  entry 🕐  Mon–Sat 9am–5pm, Sun 1pm–3pm ⏱  20–30 min

A Church of Ireland cathedral built in 1899, with a striking interior quite unlike the Gothic cathedrals seen in York and Edinburgh. The mosaic ceiling in the nave is the main draw.

  • The nave mosaic ceiling: A vast ceiling mosaic covering the entire nave vault — gold, blue, and green tessera in an abstract early-Christian style. More Byzantine than Gothic; very striking.
  • The Spire of Hope: A 40-metre stainless steel spire added in 2007 — visible from across the quarter and particularly dramatic at dusk.
  • Lord Carson’s tomb: Edward Carson, the barrister who prosecuted Oscar Wilde and who founded modern Ulster unionism, is buried in the south transept.

Giant’s Causeway Day Trip
Friday, 18 September 2026  ·  Start by 9am

Morning
🌿  UNESCO World Heritage Site — pre-book entry
Giant’s Causeway
📍  44 Causeway Road, Bushmills BT57 8SU  ·  Near Bushmills village
💲  ~£14pp  |  Book: nationaltrust.org.uk/giants-causeway 🕐  9am–6pm daily in September ⏱  Allow 2–2.5hrs on site

40,000 interlocking basalt columns formed by a volcanic eruption 60 million years ago, stepping from the cliff base down into the North Atlantic. A UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most spectacular natural landscapes in the British Isles. Nothing quite prepares you for the scale and strangeness of the columns up close.

  • The Grand Causeway: The most accessible area — a wide platform of columns at sea level. Arrive early to have this space before the tour group coaches (which typically arrive from 11am onward).
  • The Honeycomb: A section where the hexagonal columns are tightly packed and particularly regular. The geometry is extraordinary — it looks entirely artificial.
  • The Organ: A section where the columns rise 12 metres high in a curved formation resembling organ pipes. Visible from the cliff-top path above.
  • The Coastal Path: The path above the causeway gives a completely different perspective — looking down onto the columns and out to sea. Walk as far as the Chimney Stacks headland if time allows (30 min extra).
  • The Giant’s Boot: A single column worn into the shape of a giant’s footprint. Your guide map shows the location — a good detail to look for among the hundreds of columns.
Practical: The car park at the visitor centre is where you start. A shuttle bus runs from the visitor centre down to the causeway level (included in ticket). The walk down is 10–15 min — take the shuttle down and walk back up for a different perspective. Toilets are at the visitor centre only. The rocks are wet and slippery — wear shoes with grip.

Ballintoy Harbour
📍  Ballintoy, BT54 6NA  ·  5 miles east of the Giant’s Causeway on the B15
⏱  30–45 min

A tiny whitewashed harbour village a few miles east of the Causeway. Much quieter than the main visitor centre, with a small harbour café and dramatic cliff scenery. Used as a filming location for Game of Thrones (the Iron Islands).

  • Ballintoy Harbour Café: A small seasonal café at the harbour serving chowder, sandwiches, and hot drinks. Exactly the right level of simple after a morning on the causeway.
  • The harbour itself: A tiny protected bay with white-painted fishing boats and dark basalt cliffs rising behind. A good 15-min walk along the cliff path either direction.
  • Alternative — The Fullerton Arms, Ballintoy village: A pub 1 mile inland from the harbour. Good lunch menu, warm and comfortable.

🚗  Scenic drive stop
Carrick-a-Rede Rope Bridge (optional)
📍  119a Whitepark Road, Ballintoy BT54 6LS  ·  1 mile east of Ballintoy Harbour
💲  ~£10pp  |  Book: nationaltrust.org.uk/carrick-a-rede 🕐  9:30am–5:30pm in September ⏱  45–60 min

A rope bridge spanning a 30-metre chasm between the mainland cliffs and a small island, 30 metres above the sea. Originally erected by salmon fishermen in the 18th century. Pre-book — daily numbers are strictly limited.

  • The crossing: The bridge sways slightly as you cross. The drop to the sea below is vertiginous but the bridge is perfectly safe. Not recommended for anyone with significant fear of heights.
  • The island: A small rocky island with a fisherman’s cottage (now a visitor hut). The views back to the mainland cliffs from the island are the best part.
  • The cliff path: A 1km walk each way from the car park through coastal grassland. The coastal scenery is impressive even before you reach the bridge.
Note: Only do this if Carrick-a-Rede is of genuine interest and you have time. It adds 1–1.5hrs to the day. If the day is already feeling full after the Causeway and lunch, skip it and take the coastal drive back to Belfast instead.

Day 14
St George’s Market, Titanic Belfast & The Murals
Saturday, 19 September 2026  ·  Final full day
9am — Saturday market opens
🏪  Victorian market — Saturday Variety Market
St George’s Market
📍  12–20 East Bridge Street, Belfast BT1 3NQ  ·  10-min walk south of hotel
🕐  Saturday 9am–3pm — arrive before 10am Free  entry ⏱  1–1.5hrs

A beautiful Victorian covered market in continuous use since 1890, widely considered the best market in Ireland. In 2026, this day falls on a Saturday — the Variety Market. Saturday combines food stalls with arts, crafts, clothing, vintage goods, and antiques, making it the most varied market day of the week. The atmosphere is lively and the building alone is worth the visit.

  • The building: Three interconnected market halls under a glass and iron roof. The Victorian ironwork structure is remarkable — look up when you first walk in.
  • Breakfast at the market: Several stalls serve hot breakfast on Saturdays — full Ulster fry (with soda bread and potato bread, distinctly Northern Irish), pastries, and good coffee. Eat here before Titanic Belfast.
  • Food stalls: Northern Irish produce, fresh fish, artisan breads, charcuterie, and local cheeses. Saturday has slightly fewer dedicated food traders than Friday but still excellent variety.
  • Vintage and crafts: Saturday’s variety element adds clothing, vintage items, handmade crafts, jewellery, and artwork — a better browse than a Friday-only food market. Good for picking up a memorable last-day purchase.
  • Thrift note: The Saturday market regularly has vintage clothing stalls and second-hand goods alongside the crafts section. Worth a deliberate browse if vintage finds are on the agenda — the stock turns over weekly.
Route: Walk south from the hotel down Victoria Street, left onto Oxford Street, then left onto East Bridge Street. The market entrance is on the right, 10-min walk. Open until 3pm on Saturdays; the fullest and most atmospheric between 9am–noon.

10am — pre-book this slot
⚓  Museum — pre-book 10am slot
Titanic Belfast
📍  1 Olympic Way, Queen’s Road, Belfast BT3 9EP  ·  Titanic Quarter
💲  ~£27pp  |  Book: titanicbelfast.com 🕐  10am–5pm daily (last entry 3:30pm) ⏱  Allow 3 hours

Built on the exact site where the Titanic was designed, constructed, and launched, on Belfast’s Harland and Wolff shipyard. This is not a standard tourist attraction — it is genuinely one of the best museums in Europe, covering the full story from the design drawings to the wreck on the ocean floor.

  • The building (exterior): Four angular silver prows representing the four ships built on this site. The building is itself a work of architecture. Approach on foot from the car park for the full effect.
  • Gallery 1 — Belfast: Belfast at the turn of the 20th century — the industrial city that built the ship. Maps, photographs, and scale models of the shipyard.
  • Gallery 2 — The Ship: An extraordinary ride through a scale model of the Titanic’s construction — dark, loud, and immersive. Do not skip this.
  • Gallery 5 — The Sinking: The sequence of events on the night of 14 April 1912, minute by minute. Survivor testimonies and contemporary newspaper reports.
  • Gallery 9 — The Wreck: Live footage from the actual wreck, 3,821 metres below the surface. The Titanic was not found until 1985. This gallery is the most moving.
  • The SS Nomadic: The last surviving White Star Line vessel, a tender ship that transported first-class passengers to the Titanic on her maiden voyage. Berthed outside the museum — included in the ticket.
Strategy: Pre-book the 10am entry at titanicbelfast.com. This follows naturally from the market (which closes at 2pm but best done 8–10am). Allow the full 3 hours — do not rush the last two galleries. The cafe inside is good for a mid-museum coffee stop.

Afternoon — pre-book in advance
🚗  Historical tour — essential Belfast
Black Taxi Political Murals Tour
📍  Departures from city centre hotels  ·  Covers Falls Road, Shankill Road & Peace Wall
💲  ~£35–40pp  |  Book: blacktaxitours.com or Belfast Black Taxi Tours ⏱  ~1.5–2hrs

A guided tour of the murals of the Falls Road (republican) and Shankill Road (loyalist) in a black London-style taxi, driven and narrated by a local guide who lived through the Troubles. This is the single most important thing to do in Belfast to understand the city. Nothing — no book, no documentary, no museum — explains the Troubles like hearing it directly from someone who was there.

  • The Falls Road murals: Large-scale painted murals on the gable walls of republican West Belfast — political figures, historical events, international solidarity. The Bobby Sands mural on the Sinn Féin headquarters is the most visited.
  • The Shankill Road murals: The loyalist equivalent on the other side of the peace wall — a completely different visual vocabulary, different references, different history told from a different perspective.
  • The Peace Wall (Peace Line): The corrugated iron and brick wall separating republican and loyalist communities — in places 30 feet high. Taller than the Berlin Wall. You can sign it. The gates are locked at night.
  • The guide: The driver is both guide and narrator. Ask questions freely. They have heard everything and will answer honestly. This is the most important part of the experience.
Booking: Book directly at blacktaxitours.com or via your hotel concierge — these tours fill up, especially on Fridays. Ask for a pickup from the hotel (Waring Street). Tours run throughout the day; a 2pm pickup allows time after Titanic Belfast. Duration is typically 1.5–2hrs.
