Afternoon
🏭  Historic quarter — free to explore
Cathedral Quarter
📍  Cathedral Quarter, Belfast BT1  ·  Hotel front door
Free ⏱  45–60 min walk

The oldest surviving part of Belfast, centred on St Anne’s Cathedral. The narrow entries (Belfast’s historic alleyways) off Donegall Street and High Street are worth exploring. Very different character to anything seen on the trip so far.

  • The entries: Belfast’s equivalent of Edinburgh’s closes — narrow alleyways cutting between streets. Crown Entry, Pottinger’s Entry, and Joy’s Entry off High Street are the most atmospheric. Each one is different — some open onto courtyards, some onto bars, some simply turn a corner and stop.
  • Commercial Court: A cobbled square entirely covered in mosaic tiles and painted murals — one of the most distinctive spaces in Belfast. Particularly atmospheric at dusk when the bar lights come on and the square fills with people. The painted walls make it feel enclosed and intimate in a way that larger squares do not.
  • Writer’s Square: A quiet open square with a good view of St Anne’s facade. Benches in the evening sun. A good place to sit after the walk and before dinner.
  • Hill Street and Exchange Street: The Cathedral Quarter’s main independent restaurant streets. Worth a walk along both to see what is on offer for tonight — the density of good restaurants in a small area is impressive.
Romantic note: The Cathedral Quarter works best in the early evening. The afternoon tourist groups thin out, the restaurants begin to fill, and the old entries and cobbled courts take on a different character in the low September light. Wander without a plan — this is a neighbourhood that rewards not knowing where you are going.
Thrift note: Donegall Pass is Belfast’s best street for charity shops — a 10-min drive south of the hotel (or 25-min walk). A run of six or seven charity shops in quick succession, with unusually good stock. Best browsed on a Thursday afternoon when new donations come in. If thrifting is a priority on this trip, add a 45-min Donegall Pass detour before or after the Cathedral Quarter walk.

⛪  Cathedral — free
St Anne’s Cathedral (Belfast Cathedral)
📍  Donegall Street, Belfast BT1 2HB  ·  2-min walk from hotel
Free  entry 🕐  Mon–Sat 9am–5pm, Sun 1pm–3pm ⏱  20–30 min

A Church of Ireland cathedral built in 1899, with a striking interior quite unlike the Gothic cathedrals seen in York and Edinburgh. The mosaic ceiling in the nave is the main draw.

  • The nave mosaic ceiling: A vast ceiling mosaic covering the entire nave vault — gold, blue, and green tessera in an abstract early-Christian style. More Byzantine than Gothic; very striking.
  • The Spire of Hope: A 40-metre stainless steel spire added in 2007 — visible from across the quarter and particularly dramatic at dusk.
  • Lord Carson’s tomb: Edward Carson, the barrister who prosecuted Oscar Wilde and who founded modern Ulster unionism, is buried in the south transept.

Early evening — optional riverside walk
🌄  Riverside walk — free, 30 min
River Lagan & the Waterfront
📍  Lagan Weir, Belfast BT1 3LD  ·  10-min walk south from the hotel
Free ⏱  30–45 min

Walk south from the hotel along the river to the Lagan Weir and back. The Waterfront is Belfast’s most dramatic civic space — the curved Waterfront Hall on the bank, the river wide and reflective, the old dockland cranes visible downstream toward the Titanic Quarter. A quiet and surprisingly beautiful walk for a city that does not always market its waterfront well.

  • The Lagan Weir: A tidal barrage completed in 1994 that transformed the river from an industrial outflow into a clean waterway. The walkway across the weir gives views up and down the river. Good in the early evening light.
  • The Waterfront Hall: Belfast’s main concert hall on the south bank — the curved glass and stone building is architecturally interesting and the plaza outside has good views across to the Cathedral Quarter side. Check the programme — there may be an evening performance worth attending.
Romantic note: The River Lagan walk is one of those things that is not in any guidebook and is far better for it. The river at dusk, the old cranes silhouetted against the sky, the city quiet on a Thursday evening — this is a good first evening in Belfast. Walk south along the river, cross at the weir, and walk back on the other bank.

Dark Hedges, Dunluce Castle & Portstewart Strand
Friday, 18 September 2026  ·  Depart by 9am  ·  Full day on the Causeway Coast
The day: Four of Northern Ireland’s most atmospheric places, connected by one of the most scenic coastal drives in Europe. The Dark Hedges is the most beautiful natural avenue in Ireland. Dunluce Castle is one of the most dramatically sited medieval ruins in the British Isles. The Giant’s Causeway is a UNESCO-listed natural wonder. Portstewart Strand is two miles of sand at the foot of cliffs. A genuinely full and memorable day.

🌳  Natural avenue — free, one of the most beautiful places in Ireland
The Dark Hedges
📍  Bregagh Road, Armoy, County Antrim BT53 8PX  ·  Near Stanocum village
Free  entry 🕐  Always open — arrive early ⏱  Allow 30–45 min

An avenue of ancient beech trees planted in the 18th century by the Stuart family, whose intertwining branches form a vaulted tunnel over the road. The trees are now over 300 years old. Nothing prepares you for walking into it — the light filters through the branches in a way that is completely unlike anything else in Ireland. It was used as a filming location for Game of Thrones, but it existed long before that and is far more beautiful than any photograph suggests.

  • The avenue itself: Walk the full length (about 150 metres) and back at a slow pace. The best light is in the morning — September sunlight filtering through the branches at low angle is extraordinary. The road is not wide, so you are walking very close to the trees on both sides.
  • Go early: By 11am in summer and early autumn, tour coaches arrive. Before 10am on a Friday, you will often have the avenue almost to yourselves. This matters — the experience of being alone in that tunnel is entirely different from being in a crowd.
  • Photographs: The avenue is symmetrical and the perspective draws the eye down its full length. A long exposure or a slow walk produces the best images. Stand at one end and look down.
Romantic note: Walking through the Dark Hedges together in the morning light, with the branches arching overhead and the road quiet, is one of the most genuinely beautiful things on this entire trip. It is not a tourist attraction in the usual sense — it is simply a road through 300-year-old trees that happens to be extraordinary. Go slowly.

🏛  Medieval clifftop castle ruins
Dunluce Castle
📍  87 Dunluce Road, Bushmills BT57 8UY  ·  A2 Causeway Coast Road between Bushmills and Portrush
💲  ~£7pp  ·  discovernorthernireland.com 🕐  10am–5pm daily in September ⏱  Allow 1hr

A medieval castle built on a basalt stack of rock jutting out from the clifftop, connected to the mainland by a narrow bridge. Part of the castle fell into the sea in 1639 during a storm, taking the kitchen and several servants with it. It is one of the most dramatically sited buildings in Ireland — the cliffs fall away on three sides and the North Atlantic stretches to the horizon.

  • The approach: The castle is visible from the road before you reach it. Stop at the layby on the A2 for the first view — it is one of the most striking things you will see on the trip. The castle sits entirely on the stack, separated from the mainland by a narrow gorge.
  • The gatehouse: The main surviving structure — a 16th-century Scottish-style tower. Inside, the history of the MacDonnell family (the Irish lords who built it) is told through the ruins.
  • The clifftop views: From the far end of the castle, looking east along the coast toward the Causeway headlands, the view is as good as anything on the North Antrim coast. Stand at the edge — carefully — and look both ways along the cliffs.
  • The cave beneath: A sea cave runs directly beneath the castle site, used as a boat storage and escape route. Visible from the gorge path.
Romantic note: Dunluce Castle at midday on a clear September day, with the sea visible on three sides and the ruins above, is one of the most atmospheric places in Northern Ireland. Not crowded mid-week, not heavily managed — just a castle on a cliff above the sea. Allow more time than you think you need.

🌿  UNESCO World Heritage Site — pre-book entry
Giant’s Causeway
📍  44 Causeway Road, Bushmills BT57 8SU  ·  5 min from Dunluce Castle on the A2
💲  ~£14pp  |  nationaltrust.org.uk/giants-causeway 🕐  9am–6pm daily in September ⏱  Allow 1–1.5hrs (this is a stop, not the centrepiece today)

40,000 interlocking basalt columns formed by a volcanic eruption 60 million years ago, stepping from the cliff base down into the North Atlantic. A UNESCO World Heritage Site — and since you are driving straight past it, there is no reason not to stop. With Dark Hedges and Dunluce already done this morning, keep the Causeway visit focused: the Grand Causeway platform and the coastal path view above, then move on to lunch.

  • The Grand Causeway: The main platform of columns at sea level — the hexagonal geometry is genuinely extraordinary up close. Walk down to the water’s edge and stand among them. The scale only registers when you are actually there.
  • The Honeycomb: The section where the columns are most tightly packed and most regular. The most photogenic part of the site — right at the base of the Grand Causeway.
  • The cliff-top path (5 min walk up): A short climb above the columns gives the best overview of the whole site — the columns stepping down into the sea, the cliffs curving away in both directions. Worth the walk for the perspective.
  • The Organ: A section where columns rise 12 metres in a curved pipe-organ formation — visible from the cliff path above.
Practical: Pre-book a timed entry at nationaltrust.org.uk/giants-causeway. The shuttle bus from the visitor centre to the causeway level is included — take it down and walk back up via the cliff path. Toilets are at the visitor centre only. Rocks are wet and slippery — flat shoes with grip.

Afternoon — Portstewart Strand
🏖  Beach walk — free, National Trust
Portstewart Strand
📍  Strand Road, Portstewart BT55 7PG  ·  1-min drive or 15-min walk from Portstewart town
Free  to walk  |  💲  Car park: ~£5 (National Trust) 🕐  Always open ⏱  Allow 1–1.5hrs

A two-mile arc of sand backed by dunes, with the Atlantic to the north and the cliffs of the Causeway Coast visible to the east. One of the finest beaches in Ireland. In September, the summer crowds are gone entirely and the beach is almost empty — just the sand, the sea, and the view along the coast to the headlands. Drive to the end of the strand road and walk from there.

  • The length of the strand: Two miles of unbroken sand. Walk as far as you like along the beach — there is no wrong direction. The sand is firm and flat when the tide is out.
  • The dune system: Behind the beach, a large system of sand dunes managed by the National Trust. Walking into the dunes gives an entirely different view — the sea disappears and the landscape becomes quiet and enclosed.
  • The September light: The low afternoon sun in September hits the wet sand at an angle that makes it reflective and golden. This beach at 3pm in September is one of the more beautiful things on the whole trip.
  • Mussenden Temple (visible from beach): On the clifftop 2 miles east, the circular Mussenden Temple is visible from the far end of the strand — a 18th-century folly perched at the edge of a cliff. It makes a good landmark to walk toward.
Romantic note: An empty beach in September, the Atlantic in front of you, cliffs visible in both directions, and no one around. Walk to the far end and back. There is nothing complicated about why this is on the list.
Thrift note: Portstewart and Portrush both have small charity shops on their main streets worth a quick check on the way back to the car. Stock varies but turnover is good in summer resort towns at the end of the season — people donate holiday purchases before going home.

Day 14
St George’s Market, Titanic Belfast & Cave Hill
Saturday, 19 September 2026  ·  Final full day
9am — Saturday market opens
🏪  Victorian market — Saturday Variety Market
St George’s Market
📍  12–20 East Bridge Street, Belfast BT1 3NQ  ·  10-min walk south of hotel
🕐  Saturday 9am–3pm — arrive before 10am Free  entry ⏱  1–1.5hrs

A beautiful Victorian covered market in continuous use since 1890, widely considered the best market in Ireland. In 2026, this day falls on a Saturday — the Variety Market. Saturday combines food stalls with arts, crafts, clothing, vintage goods, and antiques, making it the most varied market day of the week. The atmosphere is lively and the building alone is worth the visit.

  • The building: Three interconnected market halls under a glass and iron roof. The Victorian ironwork structure is remarkable — look up when you first walk in.
  • Breakfast at the market: Several stalls serve hot breakfast on Saturdays — full Ulster fry (with soda bread and potato bread, distinctly Northern Irish), pastries, and good coffee. Eat here before Titanic Belfast.
  • Food stalls: Northern Irish produce, fresh fish, artisan breads, charcuterie, and local cheeses. Saturday has slightly fewer dedicated food traders than Friday but still excellent variety.
  • Vintage and crafts: Saturday’s variety element adds clothing, vintage items, handmade crafts, jewellery, and artwork — a better browse than a Friday-only food market. Good for picking up a memorable last-day purchase.
  • Thrift note: The Saturday market regularly has vintage clothing stalls and second-hand goods alongside the crafts section. Worth a deliberate browse if vintage finds are on the agenda — the stock turns over weekly.
Route: Walk south from the hotel down Victoria Street, left onto Oxford Street, then left onto East Bridge Street. The market entrance is on the right, 10-min walk. Open until 3pm on Saturdays; the fullest and most atmospheric between 9am–noon.
Thrift note: The Saturday Variety Market has dedicated vintage clothing and second-hand stalls in the central hall — these are worth a specific scan before moving to the food stalls. Stock turns over weekly. Also: Donegall Pass (10-min drive south) is Belfast’s best charity shop street, with five or six shops in a row. If there is time before Titanic Belfast, a quick Donegall Pass detour is worth it for anyone who loves a thrift find.

⚓  Museum — pre-book 10am slot
Titanic Belfast
📍  1 Olympic Way, Queen’s Road, Belfast BT3 9EP  ·  Titanic Quarter
💲  ~£27pp  |  Book: titanicbelfast.com 🕐  10am–5pm daily (last entry 3:30pm) ⏱  Allow 3 hours

Built on the exact site where the Titanic was designed, constructed, and launched, on Belfast’s Harland and Wolff shipyard. This is not a standard tourist attraction — it is genuinely one of the best museums in Europe, covering the full story from the design drawings to the wreck on the ocean floor.

  • The building (exterior): Four angular silver prows representing the four ships built on this site. The building is itself a work of architecture. Approach on foot from the car park for the full effect.
  • Gallery 1 — Belfast: Belfast at the turn of the 20th century — the industrial city that built the ship. Maps, photographs, and scale models of the shipyard.
  • Gallery 2 — The Ship: An extraordinary ride through a scale model of the Titanic’s construction — dark, loud, and immersive. Do not skip this.
  • Gallery 5 — The Sinking: The sequence of events on the night of 14 April 1912, minute by minute. Survivor testimonies and contemporary newspaper reports.
  • Gallery 9 — The Wreck: Live footage from the actual wreck, 3,821 metres below the surface. The Titanic was not found until 1985. This gallery is the most moving.
  • The SS Nomadic: The last surviving White Star Line vessel, a tender ship that transported first-class passengers to the Titanic on her maiden voyage. Berthed outside the museum — included in the ticket.
Strategy: Pre-book the 10am entry at titanicbelfast.com. This follows naturally from the market (which closes at 2pm but best done 8–10am). Allow the full 3 hours — do not rush the last two galleries. The cafe inside is good for a mid-museum coffee stop.

⛰  Hill walk & castle — free, best view in Belfast
Cave Hill & Belfast Castle
📍  Belfast Castle, Antrim Road, Belfast BT15 5GR  ·  North Belfast
Free  (castle exterior and grounds) 🕐  Castle open daily  ·  Hill open at all times ⏱  Allow 2–2.5hrs for walk + castle

Cave Hill is the basalt cliff and hill that rises directly above Belfast to the north. McCart’s Fort at the summit (360 metres) gives a panoramic view over the entire city, Belfast Lough, the Antrim Hills, and on a clear day across to Scotland. The hill path starts from Belfast Castle at the base. It is the finest view of Belfast that exists, and almost no tourists go there. The profile of Cave Hill is said to have inspired Jonathan Swift’s sleeping Gulliver.

  • Belfast Castle (base): A Scottish Baronial castle built in 1870 for the Earl of Shaftesbury. The castle terrace garden gives the first views over Belfast and the lough — already better than anything in the city centre. The castle has a cafe inside (open daily) for a coffee before the climb.
  • The path to McCart’s Fort: A well-marked path from the castle grounds. The full ascent takes about 45–50 minutes at a comfortable pace. The path climbs through mixed woodland before breaking out onto open grassland near the summit. Bring a layer — it is always windier at the top.
  • McCart’s Fort (summit): An ancient promontory fort at 360m. The panorama from the top is extraordinary: the whole of Belfast laid out below, the lough stretching to the Irish Sea, the Antrim Plateau to the north, and on a clear September day the Mourne Mountains to the south. The city looks entirely different from here.
  • The cave: On the descent, a series of shallow caves (Craigarusky Caves) in the basalt cliff face. One is large enough to stand in. Said to be where the United Irishmen planned the 1798 rising.
  • Belfast Castle cafe (descent): Return to the castle for tea or coffee on the terrace after the walk. The terrace has the city view without the climb — a good place to sit before driving back.
Romantic note: Standing at McCart’s Fort with Belfast spread out below, the lough glinting in the afternoon light, and the whole trip visible from above in some metaphorical sense — this is the last full afternoon of the trip and this is where to spend it. Not in a museum or on a tour bus. On a hill, looking at the city, with nowhere else to be.
Practical: The path is well-signposted from the castle car park. Walking boots or trainers with grip recommended — the upper section can be muddy after rain. The descent takes about 35 min. Total round trip from the castle to the summit and back: ~1hr 30min at a comfortable pace.
